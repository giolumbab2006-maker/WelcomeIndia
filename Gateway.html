Gateway of India

The Triumphal Arch of Mumbai

Set at the tip of Apollo Bunder adjoining the Arabian Sea, the Gateway of India is Mumbai's most distinguishing landmark. Designed by George Wittet, it served as the principal point of arrival for visitors to India and remains a symbol of the city's status as a global port.

Indo-Saracenic Design

Built from yellow Kharodi basalt, the monument is a masterpiece of the Indo-Saracenic style. It combines Gothic flamboyance with 16th-century Muslim architectural features from Gujarat. The central dome reaches an apex of 83 feet, standing as a "Arch of Triumph."

A Portal of History

Originally built to commemorate the 1911 visit of King George V, it later became a symbol of decolonization. It is famously the site where the last British troops formally left India in February 1948, marking the end of an era.

The Taj Mahal Palace Hotel

Standing majestically behind the Gateway, this hotel was built by Jamshetji Tata in 1903 as a statement of patriotic pride. After being barred from a "Europeans-only" hotel, Tata built this landmark to surpass all others, introducing Moorish domes and the finest technology to Asia.

Resilience and Significance

Despite facing the tragedy of the 2003 bombings in its vicinity, the Gateway and the Taj Mahal Palace Hotel remain standing as symbols of Mumbai's strength. Today, it is not just a tourist attraction, but the heart of India’s financial capital and a meeting point for people from all walks of life.

"A symbol of Mumbai, the financial capital of India."
