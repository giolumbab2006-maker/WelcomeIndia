Indus Valley Civilization

The Indus Valley Civilization (c. 2600–1900 BCE)

The Indus Civilization represents the earliest known urban culture of the Indian subcontinent. Of the world’s three earliest civilizations—alongside Mesopotamia and Egypt—the Indus was the most extensive, stretching from the Arabian Sea to the Yamuna River basin.

Urban Metropolises

The civilization was centered around two great cities: Harappa and Mohenjo-daro. These cities were roughly a mile square in dimension, featuring advanced political centralization and a combined population estimated at over 70,000 residents.

Agricultural Mastery

By mastering irrigated agriculture in the fertile Indus River valley, they grew wheat, barley, mustard, and sesame. They were also pioneers in cotton cultivation and the domestication of the Asian elephant for its ivory.

Pioneers of Engineering

Identified first in 1921, the ruins of Mohenjo-daro (a UNESCO World Heritage site) reveal a society that could control formidable annual floods while utilizing them to fertilize the plains. Their sophisticated urban layout remains a benchmark for early human engineering and social organization.

Trade and Expansion

From the Shimla Hills to the Gulf of Khambhat, the Indus people established over 100 towns and villages. Their economy was primarily farming-based, supplemented by a vast network of commerce that connected the northern hills to the southern coasts, creating a secure foothold that allowed their population to flourish for nearly a millennium.
