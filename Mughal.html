Establishment of the Mughal Empire

The Mughal Empire (1526 – 1857)

The Mughal Dynasty was a line of Muslim rulers of Turkic-Mongol origin who transformed the Indian subcontinent. At its zenith, the empire was a global superpower, accounting for nearly 24% of the world’s GDP and fostering a unique cultural integration of Hindu and Muslim traditions.

A "Gunpowder Empire"

Alongside the Ottomans and Safavids, the Mughals were a "Gunpowder Empire." Their success relied on the effective use of firearms, artillery, and a centralized military organization that allowed them to unify diverse regions into a single political framework.

Economic Supremacy

By the late 17th century, under rulers like Akbar and Aurangzeb, the empire stretched from the Bay of Bengal to the Arabian Sea. With a population of up to 150 million, it became one of the richest economies in history, influencing global trade and industry.

Babur: The Founder

The dynasty was founded by Babur, a descendant of both Timur and Genghis Khan. After conquering Kabul, he turned his sights toward Hindustan. In 1526, at the First Battle of Panipat, Babur routed the forces of Ibrahim Lodi, securing the keys to Delhi and Agra and laying the foundation for an empire that would endure for over three centuries.

Legacy of Integration

While Babur established the empire, it was his grandson Akbar who consolidated it through inclusive governance. The Mughals replaced simple landownership with a system of loyalty to the crown, using Persian as the official language and creating an administrative structure that remained influential until the British deposition of Bahadur Shah II in 1857.
